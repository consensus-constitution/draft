# Outreach Kit

Three templates, one strategy: **lead with a mechanism, not the manifesto.** Experts decline to "review a constitution"; they cannot resist breaking a specific machine in their own field. Every message asks one narrow question and links the repo for those who go deeper.

---

## 1. Scholar email (adapt per recipient's specialty)

**Subject options:**
- A suspensive-veto design calibrated against a 60% legislative threshold — where does it break?
- Multi-member STV at 1:240,000 with a 3–7 magnitude bound — the failure modes I can't see
- [Their specialty] question from a complete constitutional redraft

**Body:**

> Professor [Name],
>
> Your work on [specific paper/book — one line showing you actually read it] is why I'm writing to you rather than generally.
>
> I've completed a full working redraft of the U.S. Constitution — ten articles, 96 individually logged design decisions — built as a design exercise in the spirit of the National Constitution Center's Constitution Drafting Project. It was drafted in structured collaboration with an AI assistant; every decision was mine and each is logged with its reasoning. I'm disclosing that up front.
>
> I'm not asking you to review the document. I'm asking one question in your field:
>
> **[THE QUESTION — examples below]**
>
> The provision, its drafter's note, and the decision log are three clicks deep here: [repo link, anchored to the section]. If the mechanism survives you, that's worth knowing; if it doesn't, the failure gets logged with your name on the find, if you want it there.
>
> Thirty minutes of your skepticism would improve this more than a hundred hours of agreement.
>
> [Name]

**Example questions by specialty:**
- *Electoral systems (FairVote, Elkins/Ginsburg, EndFPTP academics):* "Art. I §2 bounds district magnitude at 3–7 to block magnitude-gerrymandering. Is the bound sufficient, or does a hostile commission still have a proportionality exploit I haven't found?"
- *Legislation/Congress scholars:* "Art. I §3(c) demotes the Senate to a 12-month suspensive role with a 65% House override, while §5 forces floor votes. What's the worst-case interaction — can a majority leader still bury the override vote?"
- *Presidential power:* "Art. I §4(c) lets emergency measures take effect at simple majority for 90 days, non-renewable in substance. Does the 'substantially the same' bar actually hold against serial re-labeling?"
- *Criminal procedure:* "Art. VI §7(f) makes over-detention compensation non-waivable to stop plea-laundering, but a plea to exactly time-served still zeroes it. Is that channel closable without compensating the convicted?"
- *Privacy/surveillance (EFF, EPIC, academic):* "Art. VIII §5(c) walls foreign-intelligence collection off from domestic use. Does the wall survive a Five Eyes-style reciprocal arrangement?"
- *Federalism:* "Art. V §4 constitutionalizes Trail Smelter domestically — no measurable cross-boundary harm absent compact. Does 'measurable' collapse the rule at continental scale?"

## 3. The institutional pitch (NCC / ASU Center for Constitutional Design / law school centers)

One paragraph, sent with the annotated edition attached:

> The Constitution Drafting Project proved that team-drafted ideal constitutions surface unexpected consensus. This submission tests a different method: a single citizen deliberating decision-by-decision with an AI assistant, every choice logged and auditable — 96 decisions across ten articles, from an STV House and a suspensive Senate through the first social guarantees drafted for an American text. I'm offering it to [institution] not as scholarship but as a specimen: of citizen constitutional design, of human-AI deliberation, and of an annotation method that makes a draft's reasoning fully inspectable. If it's useful as a foil, a teaching document, or a stress-test target for your scholars, it's yours — it's public domain.

## Sequencing

1. **Week 1–2:** Five scholar emails, each a different article, each a different specialty. Wait for at least two responses before going public — their finds become your credibility ("already patched per Prof. X").
2. **Week 3:** Community posts (r/EndFPTP first; it's the most rigorous and the most sympathetic to the subject).
3. **Week 4+:** Institutional pitch, citing the review activity already visible in the repo's Issues tab.

The repo's Issues tab is the portfolio. Every serious engagement visible there makes the next expert likelier to engage.
